# Don't Panic
## How a Girls' School Built Its Own AI — and Then Got Out of the Way
### The Study · Apple Education Event · May 4, 2026

**Format:** ~10 min Amalia + 5–10 min students
**Audience:** IT and pedagogical directors
**Goal:** Visibility for The Study's work in AI; a transferable model peer schools can adopt
**Speaker:** Amalia Liogas, Director of IT, The Study (with two students)
**Through-line:** *From Information to Imagination — and what it takes to teach in the second one.*

---

## Slide 1 — Title

**Don't Panic.**

*A field guide to AI at The Study.*

> **Speaker notes (15 sec):** Walk on. Pause. Let the room settle. Smile.
> "Good morning. It's the fourth of May. So before anything else — *may the future be with you.*"
> One beat for the laugh. Then directly into Slide 2.

> **Visual:** Black slide. White serif title. Small "42" in the corner. No logo clutter.

---

## Slide 2 — The Question Most Schools Are Asking

> *"Should AI be in our school?"*

> **Speaker notes (45 sec):** "I want to be honest with you. Most schools right now are stuck on a single question: *should AI be in our school?* It's a fine question. It is also the wrong question."
>
> "We stopped asking it about eighteen months ago. Because while we were debating whether AI belonged in education, our students were already using it. The question wasn't *whether*. The question was *whose* AI, on *whose* terms, in *whose* hands."

> **Visual:** White text on black. The question in quotation marks, large.

---

## Slide 3 — The Question We Asked Instead

> *What happens when girls build it themselves?*

> **Speaker notes (30 sec):** Slow down here. Let the slide land.
>
> "At The Study — an all-girls' school in Montreal — we asked a different question. Not *should AI be in girls' education*, but *what happens when girls build it themselves?*"
>
> "But before I show you what we found out, I have to tell you what era we think we're in. Because that changes everything."

> **Visual:** Same format as previous slide. Visual rhyme intentional.

---

## Slide 4 — From Information to Imagination

**The age that built our schools is ending.**
*The one our students will inherit has begun.*

> **Speaker notes (75 sec):** "The schools we run today were designed for the **Information Age**. Their job was *access* — getting students near books, near data, near experts. We did that job. We won. Information is now infinite, instant, and free."
>
> "Which means the scarce resource of the next century is no longer information. It is **imagination**. The ability to look at infinite information and make something that did not exist before. That is the **Imagination Age**. And whether we like it or not, it has already started."
>
> "AI is not the enemy of learning. AI is the *catalyst* that ends the Information Age and forces education to become what it should have been all along — not the storage of facts, but the practice of creation."
>
> "Everything I'm about to show you is what that looks like inside a girls' school in Montreal."

> **Visual:** Two words, one fading into the other across the slide: ***Information* → *Imagination***. Serif type. Slow transition. Nothing else on screen.

---

## Slide 5 — Meet Rosie

**Rosie**
*Our foundation. So our students don't need one.*

> **Speaker notes (90 sec):** "First — we built our own. Her name is Rosie. She lives on our campus, on our infrastructure, powered by Sage.is. But I want to be precise about *why* we built her, because most schools get this backwards."
>
> "We did not build Rosie so our students would have to use it. We built Rosie so our students would *never have to be locked into anything else.*"
>
> "Three things matter. **One:** she's on-site. Our data stays with us. **Two:** she's transparent. Teachers shape what she does. **Three** — and this is the one I want IT directors in the room to hear — **she is the foundation that lets our students freely use everything else.** Lovable today. Claude Code, OpenAI Codex tomorrow. Our own models when we want them. The point of owning your foundation is that you stop having to fight about the floor above it."

> **Visual:** A clean photograph or simple rendering of Rosie's interface. One UI, one moment.

---

## Slide 6 — What the Girls Built

**SafeTrace**
*Built by students, on the tool they chose, to solve a problem grown-ups missed.*

> **Speaker notes (90 sec):** "I want to introduce you to a project two of our students built. It's called SafeTrace. You upload a photo — the kind of photo a teenager might post — and SafeTrace tells you what you've accidentally revealed. Your school crest. The street sign in the background. The reflection in the window. It's an AI tool that detects self-doxxing. Built by girls, for girls, to address a problem girls face disproportionately."
>
> "They built it on **Lovable**. Other students at our school are building on **Claude Code**. Some are using **OpenAI Codex**. Some are working directly with our own models on Rosie. **We do not dictate the stack to our students.** We give them a sovereign foundation — and we trust them to choose the tool that fits the imagination."
>
> "I am going to stop talking now. The people who built SafeTrace are better at explaining it. [Student names], come up here."

> **Visual:** SafeTrace logo / interface. One anonymized before/after example showing what the tool flags.

---

## Slide 7 — [STUDENT SEGMENT · 5–10 MIN]

**The girls take the room.**

> **Speaker notes for Amalia (handoff):** Step aside. Let them own it. Resist the urge to fill silence or correct technical detail.

> **Guidance for the students (frame, not script):**
> - Open with *why* you built it — what problem you noticed in your own life or your friends' lives.
> - Show it. A live demo if Wi-Fi cooperates; a recorded demo if it doesn't.
> - Tell us *which* tool you chose and *why* you chose it. (This is the part the IT directors most want to hear from you.)
> - Tell us one thing you learned that surprised you.
> - Tell us one thing you want adults to understand about how your generation actually uses AI.
> - End with what you would build *next* if no one stopped you.

> **Visual:** Live demo screen. Or — fallback — a 60-second cut of the tool in action with the students' voiceover.

---

## Slide 8 — The Pattern

**Tool fluency, not tool loyalty.**
*The girls will replace every tool on this slide before they graduate. Good.*

> **Speaker notes (75 sec):** Take the mic back. Thank the students by name. Then:
>
> "Last year's class built remarkable things on Rosie — and then, like every senior class, they graduated. That's how schools work. The projects don't compound the way they do in a company."
>
> "What compounds is the *posture*. The students you just heard from will use a tool next year that doesn't exist yet. The class after them will replace it again. **This is what Imagination Age literacy looks like.** Not loyalty to a tool. The ability to *choose* one — fluently, fearlessly, on the merits."

> **Visual:** Two columns. *Tools come and go* (logos: Lovable, Claude Code, OpenAI Codex, "...") | *The posture stays* (a single sentence: "Build real things. Choose your tools. Show your work.")

---

## Slide 9 — The Transferable Model

**Three things you can do Monday.**

> **Speaker notes (45 sec):** "I know who is in this room. Heads of IT. Directors of pedagogy. People who are going to fly home tonight and field the same question on Monday morning: *what are we doing about AI?* So I want to leave you with three things you can actually do."
>
> Walk through them slowly. Land each one.

---

## Slide 10 — One

**Own your foundation. Free everything above it.**

> **Speaker notes (75 sec):** "Your AI strategy has two layers, and they should be answered very differently."
>
> "**The foundation** — your institutional AI, your data, your operations. *Own it.* On-site. Open-source if you can. Sovereign. So your students never have to negotiate with a vendor for the right to learn." Ours is Rosie, powered by Sage.is. I want to know what yours will be.
>
> "**Everything above it** — the tools your students pick to build their own projects. *Free them.* Let them use Lovable. Let them use Claude Code. Let them use what comes after Claude Code. Your job is not to pick the tool for them. Your job is to build the floor solid enough that they can stand on it and imagine anything."

> **Visual:** Two stacked layers. *Foundation: yours.* / *Everything above: theirs.* Simple line diagram.

---

## Slide 11 — Two

**Let students build, with the tools they pick.**
*The most powerful curriculum is the one they write themselves.*

> **Speaker notes (45 sec):** "Most AI-in-school conversations are about how to *prevent* students from using AI. Flip it. The most important literacy your students will gain is from *building* with AI — and from *choosing* the tool that fits the problem. The Imagination Age does not reward students who memorized the right answer. It rewards students who can ask a better question."

> **Visual:** A real photograph of Study students at work — not stock.

---

## Slide 12 — Three

**Make girls' voices the default, not the diversity slide.**

> **Speaker notes (75 sec):** "This last one is the one I want you to take seriously. The Information Age was built — mostly — by visible men and invisible women. We can argue about why. We cannot argue about whether."
>
> "The Imagination Age is being built right now. The next decade of AI will be shaped by the people who are in the room when it's made. If we are not deliberate, that room will look exactly like the rooms that built the last era of technology. We do not have to repeat that mistake."
>
> "Whatever school you run, whatever room you control — the question is not whether your girls are interested in AI. They are. The question is whether you have built a place where they can imagine *with* it."

> **Visual:** A single sentence on a black slide. No image. Let it sit.

---

## Slide 13 — The Close

**The answer is 42.**
*We're still working on the question.*

> **Speaker notes (60 sec):** "I'll close with this. There is a number on our school's project site — 42. Some of you will know where it comes from. *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* tells us 42 is the answer to life, the universe, and everything. The joke is that no one ever figured out the question."
>
> "I think the Information Age was the same. We spent a century gathering answers — the tools, the models, the infrastructure. We have them now. The question is what we'll *imagine* with them. And I think the people most likely to find that question are the ones in our classrooms right now, building things we didn't ask them to build."
>
> Pause. Look up.
>
> "May the future be with you. Thank you."

> **Visual:** Black slide. Just **42** in a serif typeface. Hold for the applause.

## Appendix — Practical Notes

### Runtime
- Amalia segments: ~10 min (Imagination Age frame adds ~75 sec to original)
- Student segment: 5–10 min
- **Total: 15–20 min**

### Tech requirements
- Live demo backup: pre-recorded SafeTrace walkthrough (~60 sec) in case Wi-Fi fails
- Slide remote with confidence monitor (Apple events typically provide)
- Lavalier mic for Amalia, handheld for students

### Things NOT to include
- No "AI revolution" language
- No fear-mongering about competitors or peer schools
- No vendor pitches (Sage.is is named twice, both in context, and we move on)
- No reading from slides
- No apologizing for being a small school

### Sensitivity / pre-flight checklist
- Confirm student and family consent for the student segment and any anonymized images
- Confirm SafeTrace demo doesn't surface any real personal data
- Confirm The Study comms team has reviewed the messaging
- Confirm tool names (Lovable, Claude Code, OpenAI Codex) are acceptable for an Apple-hosted venue

### Cocktail-party sentence (the line you want repeated on the train home)
> *We didn't wait to see if AI belonged in girls' education. We built the foundation, and we let the girls imagine what to build on top of it.*

### Theme map (where the through-line lands)
- **Slide 4** — explicit reframe: Information Age → Imagination Age
- **Slide 6** — *"the tool that fits the imagination"* (subtle thread)
- **Slide 8** — *"This is what Imagination Age literacy looks like."*
- **Slide 10** — *"stand on it and imagine anything"*
- **Slide 11** — *"a better question"* (vs. memorizing answers)
- **Slide 12** — explicit second mention: who builds the Imagination Age
- **Slide 13** — closing callback: *"what we'll imagine with them"*
